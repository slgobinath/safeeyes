## The 20-20-20 Rule

The **20-20-20 rule** is one of the simplest and most effective strategies for reducing digital eye strain. It was popularized by Dr. Jeffrey Anshel and is widely recommended by ophthalmologists worldwide.

### What Is It?

Every **20 minutes**, look at something **20 feet** (approximately 6 meters) away for at least **20 seconds**.

### Why It Works

- **Reduces accommodative spasm**: Your eyes' focusing muscles (ciliary muscles) can cramp after prolonged near-focus. Looking at a distant object allows these muscles to relax.
- **Restores blink rate**: People blink about 66% less while looking at screens. The 20-second break gives your eyes a chance to re-moisten.
- **Decreases fatigue**: Regular breaks prevent the cumulative fatigue that leads to headaches, blurred vision, and burning eyes.

### How to Implement It

1. **Use Safe Eyes**: Configure Safe Eyes to remind you every 20 minutes to take a break. This is exactly what the application is designed for!
2. **Set a timer**: If you cannot use Safe Eyes, use a phone timer or a browser extension.
3. **Look out a window**: A window view is ideal because it provides a natural, distant focal point.
4. **Focus on a distant object**: Pick a specific object 20+ feet away—a tree, a building, or a sign—and focus on it.
5. **Blink actively**: During the 20-second break, make a conscious effort to blink fully and slowly several times.

### Tips for Making It a Habit

- Start with shorter intervals (e.g., every 10 minutes) and gradually increase.
- Pair the break with standing up or stretching for double benefit.
- Don't skip breaks just because you're "in the zone"—eye strain sneaks up on you.

## Ergonomics

Proper ergonomics play a crucial role in minimizing eye strain and overall physical discomfort during extended screen use.

### Monitor Position

| Setting | Recommendation |
|---------|---------------|
| **Distance** | 20–26 inches (50–65 cm) from your eyes |
| **Height** | Top of the screen at or slightly below eye level |
| **Tilt** | Screen tilted back 10–20 degrees |
| **Position** | Directly in front of you, centered |

### Why Monitor Position Matters

- **Too close**: Forces your eyes to work harder to focus, increasing strain.
- **Too high**: Causes you to open your eyes wider, leading to dry eyes and neck strain.
- **Too low**: Promotes slouching and neck pain.
- **Off-center**: Creates uneven visual demand and neck torsion.

### Keyboard and Desk Setup

- **Keyboard height**: Elbows should be at roughly 90 degrees when typing.
- **Desk depth**: Ensure at least 20 inches of desk depth between you and the monitor.
- **Document holder**: If referencing documents, place them at the same height and distance as the screen to avoid constant refocusing.

### Chair and Posture

- Sit with your back fully supported by the chair's backrest.
- Feet should be flat on the floor or on a footrest.
- Shoulders should be relaxed, not hunched.
- Wrists should be neutral (not bent up or down) while typing.

### Lighting and Glare

- **Avoid working in the dark**: A completely dark room with a bright screen creates extreme contrast that strains the eyes.
- **Use indirect lighting**: Position your light source so it doesn't shine directly on the screen or into your eyes.
- **Eliminate glare**: Use an anti-glare screen filter if needed, and position your monitor perpendicular to windows.
- **Window management**: Close blinds or curtains to prevent direct sunlight on your screen. Consider using a matte screen protector.

### Dual Monitor Setup

- Place the primary monitor directly in front of you.
- Place the secondary monitor at the same distance and height, immediately adjacent.
- If you use both monitors equally, center them both with a slight angle.

## Screen Settings

Optimizing your screen settings can dramatically reduce eye strain without any change in your work habits.

### Brightness

- **Match ambient light**: Your screen brightness should roughly match the brightness of your surrounding workspace. A good test: a white document on screen should look like a white piece of paper held next to the screen.
- **Too bright**: Causes glare and makes you squint, leading to headaches.
- **Too dim**: Forces your eyes to work harder, causing fatigue.
- **Auto-brightness**: Enable automatic brightness adjustment if your device supports it, so the screen adapts as lighting conditions change throughout the day.

### Color Temperature

- **Blue light**: Screens emit high levels of blue light, which contributes to eye strain and can disrupt your circadian rhythm (sleep-wake cycle).
- **Warm tones at night**: Use warmer (more yellow/amber) color temperatures in the evening to reduce blue light exposure.
- **Tools**:
  - **Safe Eyes** can help remind you to enable night mode.
  - **f.lux** (cross-platform): Automatically adjusts color temperature based on time of day.
  - **Redshift** (Linux): Similar to f.lux for Linux users.
  - **Night Light** (Windows) / **Night Shift** (macOS): Built-in OS features.

### Contrast

- Use **high contrast** between text and background (dark text on light background is generally preferred).
- Avoid low-contrast color schemes (e.g., light gray text on white background).
- If using dark mode, ensure text is bright enough to read comfortably without increasing brightness.

### Text Size and Scaling

- **Increase text size** if you find yourself leaning in to read.
- Use a minimum of **12pt font** for comfortable reading on screen.
- Adjust your OS display scaling (e.g., 125% or 150%) rather than sitting closer to the screen.
- In your web browser, use Ctrl+/Ctrl- or Cmd+/Cmd- to adjust page zoom as needed.

### Refresh Rate

- Use the **highest refresh rate** your monitor supports (60 Hz minimum, 120+ Hz preferred).
- Lower refresh rates can cause imperceptible flicker that contributes to eye strain and headaches.
- Check your display settings to ensure you're not running at a lower refresh rate than your monitor supports.

### Multiple Screens

- Ensure all monitors have **consistent brightness and color settings**.
- Mismatched screens force your eyes to constantly readjust, increasing fatigue.

## Long-Term Habits

Building sustainable long-term habits is the key to preserving your eye health over years and decades of screen use.

### Regular Eye Exams

- **Schedule annual comprehensive eye exams** with an optometrist or ophthalmologist.
- Inform your doctor about your screen usage habits—they can check for computer vision syndrome specifically.
- **Update your prescription** regularly. An outdated prescription forces your eyes to work harder.
- Consider **computer glasses** with anti-reflective coating if you spend 4+ hours daily on screens, even if you don't normally need corrective lenses.

### Blink Awareness and Eye Moisture

- **Normal blink rate**: 15–20 blinks per minute.
- **Screen blink rate**: Often drops to 5–7 blinks per minute.
- Practice **conscious blinking**: Every few minutes, do 3–5 full, slow blinks.
- Use **lubricating eye drops** (artificial tears) if your eyes feel dry, especially in air-conditioned or heated environments.
- Avoid drops with redness removers—they can cause rebound redness with long-term use.
- Consider a **humidifier** in your workspace to maintain ambient moisture.

### The 20-20-20 Rule as a Lifelong Practice

- Make the 20-20-20 rule **non-negotiable**, just like brushing your teeth.
- Use **Safe Eyes** as your permanent companion—it's designed exactly for this purpose.
- Over time, your body will naturally start wanting breaks—listen to those signals.

### Nutrition for Eye Health

- **Omega-3 fatty acids** (found in fish, flaxseed, walnuts): Support tear production and reduce dry eye symptoms.
- **Lutein and Zeaxanthin** (found in leafy greens, eggs, corn): Protect the macula from blue light damage.
- **Vitamin A** (found in carrots, sweet potatoes, spinach): Essential for retinal function.
- **Vitamin C and E** (found in citrus fruits, nuts, berries): Antioxidants that protect against oxidative stress in the eyes.
- **Stay hydrated**: Dehydration reduces tear production. Aim for 8 glasses of water daily.

### Exercise and Movement

- **Regular physical exercise** improves blood circulation to the eyes.
- **Eye exercises**:
  - **Palming**: Rub your hands together to warm them, then gently cup them over your closed eyes for 30 seconds. Breathe deeply.
  - **Figure-8**: Imagine a large figure-8 about 10 feet in front of you. Trace it with your eyes slowly, first one direction, then the other.
  - **Near-far focus**: Hold your thumb 10 inches from your face and focus on it for 3–5 seconds, then focus on something 20+ feet away for 3–5 seconds. Repeat 10 times.
  - **Eye rolling**: Slowly roll your eyes in a circle, first clockwise, then counterclockwise. Repeat 5 times each direction.

### Sleep and Recovery

- **Aim for 7–9 hours of sleep** per night. Your eyes recover and repair during sleep.
- **Avoid screens 1 hour before bed**: Blue light suppresses melatonin production, making it harder to fall asleep and reducing sleep quality.
- If you must use screens before bed, use the warmest possible color temperature setting.

### Digital Detox

- **Schedule screen-free time** each day—even 30 minutes makes a difference.
- **Screen-free weekends**: Try designating part of your weekend as completely screen-free.
- **Hobbies away from screens**: Reading physical books, outdoor activities, crafts, cooking—all give your eyes a much-needed break.

### Watch for Warning Signs

Consult an eye care professional if you experience any of the following persistently:

- Blurred or double vision
- Frequent headaches
- Burning, itching, or red eyes
- Increased sensitivity to light
- Difficulty focusing
- Neck or shoulder pain that may be related to screen use

## Quick Reference Checklist

Use this daily checklist to maintain healthy screen habits:

- [ ] Safe Eyes is running and configured for 20-minute break intervals
- [ ] Monitor is 20–26 inches from my eyes
- [ ] Top of screen is at or below eye level
- [ ] Screen brightness matches ambient lighting
- [ ] Blue light filter / night mode enabled in the evening
- [ ] Text size is comfortable without leaning in
- [ ] No glare on my screen from windows or lights
- [ ] I'm taking a 20-second break every 20 minutes
- [ ] I'm blinking consciously during screen use
- [ ] I'm drinking enough water throughout the day
- [ ] I've done eye exercises at least once today
- [ ] I'll avoid screens for at least 1 hour before bed tonight
