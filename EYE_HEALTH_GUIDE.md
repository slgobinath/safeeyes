## The 20-20-20 Rule

The **20-20-20 rule** is one of the simplest and most effective strategies for reducing digital eye strain. It was developed by Dr. Jeffrey Anshel and is widely recommended by optometrists worldwide.

### What Is the 20-20-20 Rule?

Every **20 minutes**, look at something **20 feet** (approximately 6 meters) away for at least **20 seconds**.

### Why It Works

- **Reduces accommodative stress**: Your eyes' ciliary muscles are constantly engaged when focusing on near objects (like a screen). Looking at a distant object allows these muscles to relax.
- **Restores blink rate**: Studies show that people blink 66% less frequently when using screens. The 20-second break encourages natural blinking, which re-lubricates the ocular surface.
- **Decreases tear evaporation**: Prolonged screen viewing leads to incomplete blinking and faster tear film breakup. Regular breaks help maintain a stable tear film.

### How to Implement It

1. **Use SafeEyes**: Configure SafeEyes to remind you every 20 minutes to take a break.
2. **Set a timer**: If you don't have SafeEyes, use a phone or desktop timer.
3. **Pick a distant focal point**: Identify an object 20+ feet away before you start working (a window, a clock on a far wall, a tree outside).
4. **Look and blink**: During the break, consciously blink several times while gazing at the distant object.
5. **Don't skip it**: Even if you're in the middle of a task, the 20-second pause will not hurt your productivity — it will improve it by reducing fatigue.

### Variations

- **20-20-20+**: For added benefit, stand up and stretch during the 20-second break.
- **10-10-10 for children**: Younger users may benefit from a 10-minute interval with 10 seconds of distance viewing.

## Ergonomics

Proper ergonomics play a crucial role in minimizing eye strain and musculoskeletal discomfort during screen use.

### Monitor Position

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 20–26 inches (50–65 cm) from your eyes |
| **Height** | Top of the screen at or slightly below eye level |
| **Tilt** | Slightly tilted back (10–20 degrees) |
| **Angle** | Directly in front of you; no more than 30° off-center |

### Desk and Chair Setup

- **Chair height**: Feet flat on the floor, thighs parallel to the ground.
- **Desk height**: Elbows at approximately 90° when typing.
- **Keyboard position**: At a distance that allows relaxed shoulders — avoid reaching.
- **Document holder**: Place reference materials at the same height and distance as your monitor to avoid constant refocusing.

### Lighting

- **Avoid glare**: Position your monitor perpendicular to windows. Do not have a window directly behind the monitor or behind you reflecting on the screen.
- **Use ambient lighting**: Avoid working in a dark room with only the screen as a light source. The room should be moderately lit.
- **Task lighting**: Use a desk lamp with a diffuser for reading printed material, but ensure it doesn't create glare on the screen.
- **Anti-glare screens**: Consider a matte screen protector if glare cannot be eliminated through positioning.

### Dual Monitor Setup

- Place the primary monitor directly in front of you.
- Place the secondary monitor at the same height and distance, angled slightly inward.
- If you use one monitor 80% of the time, center it and place the other to the side.
- Minimize head turning — move your eyes, not your neck.

### Laptop Use

- Avoid using laptops on your lap for extended periods — the screen is too low.
- Use a laptop stand to raise the screen to proper eye level.
- Pair with an external keyboard and mouse to maintain proper arm position.

## Screen Settings

Adjusting your screen settings can significantly reduce eye fatigue without any cost.

### Brightness

- **Match ambient light**: Your screen brightness should roughly match the brightness of your surrounding workspace. A screen that is too bright or too dim causes your eyes to work harder.
- **Auto-brightness**: Enable automatic brightness adjustment if your device supports it.
- **Time of day**: Reduce brightness in the evening and increase it in well-lit daytime conditions.

### Color Temperature

- **Blue light filter**: Use built-in blue light reduction features (Night Light on Windows, Night Shift on macOS, or SafeEyes' own break reminders).
- **Warm tones at night**: Shift to warmer color temperatures (2700K–3000K) 2–3 hours before bedtime to support your circadian rhythm.
- **Daytime settings**: During the day, a cooler temperature (5000K–6500K) is acceptable, but avoid excessively blue/cool settings.

### Contrast and Text

- **High contrast**: Use dark text on a light background or light text on a dark background. Avoid low-contrast color combinations.
- **Font size**: Set your default font size to at least 14pt for comfortable reading. If you find yourself squinting, increase it.
- **Font choice**: Use sans-serif fonts (like Arial, Helvetica, or Calibri) for on-screen reading — they render more cleanly at lower resolutions.
- **Line spacing**: Increase line spacing to 1.5× for long-form reading to reduce visual crowding.

### Refresh Rate

- **Minimum 60 Hz**: Ensure your monitor runs at 60 Hz or higher. Flicker at lower refresh rates can cause headaches and eye strain, even if you don't consciously notice the flicker.
- **Higher is better**: 120 Hz or 144 Hz displays are noticeably smoother and may reduce fatigue during prolonged use.
- **Check your settings**: Many monitors default to 60 Hz but support higher rates. Check your display settings.

### Screen Resolution and Scaling

- **Native resolution**: Always use your monitor's native resolution for the sharpest image.
- **Display scaling**: If text is too small at native resolution, use your OS's display scaling feature rather than lowering resolution.
- **Subpixel rendering**: Ensure ClearType (Windows) or font smoothing (macOS) is enabled.

### Dark Mode

- Dark mode can reduce eye strain in low-light environments.
- However, in bright environments, dark mode may cause more strain due to the high contrast between the dark screen and bright surroundings.
- Choose the mode that feels most comfortable for your current lighting conditions.

## Long-Term Habits

Building sustainable habits is the key to preserving your vision over years and decades of screen use.

### Regular Eye Exams

- **Schedule annual comprehensive eye exams** with an optometrist or ophthalmologist.
- **Discuss your screen usage** with your eye care professional — they can recommend computer-specific glasses if needed.
- **Update your prescription** regularly. Even small changes in your prescription can cause significant eye strain when using screens for hours.
- **Ask about blue-light-blocking lenses** if you experience sleep disruption from screen use.

### The 20-20-20 Rule as a Lifelong Habit

- Integrate the rule into your daily workflow permanently, not just when your eyes hurt.
- Use SafeEyes or similar tools to automate the reminders.
- Encourage your coworkers and family members to adopt the practice.

### Hydration and Nutrition

- **Stay hydrated**: Dehydration reduces tear production, leading to dry eyes. Drink at least 8 glasses of water daily.
- **Omega-3 fatty acids**: Found in fish, flaxseed, and walnuts, omega-3s support tear film quality.
- **Vitamin A**: Essential for retinal health. Found in carrots, sweet potatoes, and leafy greens.
- **Lutein and zeaxanthin**: These antioxidants accumulate in the retina and protect against blue light damage. Found in kale, spinach, and eggs.
- **Vitamin C and E**: Help maintain the health of blood vessels in the eyes.

### Exercise and the Outdoors

- **Spend time outdoors**: Studies suggest that 2+ hours of outdoor time per day can reduce the risk of myopia progression, especially in children and young adults.
- **Eye exercises**: While evidence is mixed, some people find relief with:
  - **Palming**: Cover your eyes with warm palms for 30 seconds to relax them.
  - **Figure-eight**: Trace an imaginary figure-eight with your eyes, 10 times in each direction.
  - **Near-far focus**: Alternate focus between a pencil at arm's length and a distant object, 10–15 times.
- **General exercise**: Cardiovascular exercise improves blood circulation to the eyes.

### Digital Detox

- **Screen-free evenings**: Try to avoid screens 1–2 hours before bed.
- **Screen-free weekends**: Dedicate portions of your weekend to non-screen activities.
- **Mindful usage**: Before opening your phone or laptop, ask yourself: "Is this necessary right now?"

### Manage Dry Eyes

- **Use artificial tears**: Over-the-counter lubricating eye drops (preservative-free) can help during long sessions.
- **Humidifier**: Use a humidifier in dry environments to prevent tear evaporation.
- **Blink consciously**: Make a habit of fully blinking — many screen users develop a habit of partial blinking.
- **Avoid direct airflow**: Don't sit directly in front of fans or air conditioning vents.

### Sleep Hygiene

- **7–9 hours of sleep**: Your eyes need rest to recover from the day's strain.
- **Limit late-night screen use**: Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production, making it harder to fall asleep.
- **Use blue light filters** in the evening if you must use screens.

### Monitor Your Symptoms

Pay attention to these warning signs and consult an eye care professional if you experience them persistently:

- Blurred or double vision
- Persistent dry or watery eyes
- Headaches after screen use
- Neck or shoulder pain (often related to screen ergonomics)
- Increased light sensitivity
- Difficulty focusing between near and far objects

## Quick Reference Checklist

Use this daily checklist to maintain healthy eyes:

- [ ] Take a 20-20-20 break every 20 minutes (use SafeEyes!)
- [ ] Monitor is 20–26 inches from eyes
- [ ] Top of screen at or below eye level
- [ ] Screen brightness matches room lighting
- [ ] Blue light filter enabled in the evening
- [ ] Blink consciously during screen use
- [ ] Stay hydrated throughout the day
- [ ] Use artificial tears if eyes feel dry
- [ ] Get 7–9 hours of sleep
- [ ] Schedule an annual eye exam
- [ ] Spend at least 2 hours outdoors
