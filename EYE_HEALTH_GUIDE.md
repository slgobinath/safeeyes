## The 20-20-20 Rule

The 20-20-20 rule is the most widely recommended practice by ophthalmologists for reducing digital eye strain. It is simple, effective, and easy to remember:

### What Is the 20-20-20 Rule?

Every **20 minutes**, look at something **20 feet** (approximately 6 meters) away for at least **20 seconds**.

### Why It Works

- **Reduces accommodation spasm**: Your eyes' ciliary muscles are constantly contracted when focusing on near objects (like a screen). Looking at a distant object allows these muscles to relax.
- **Restores blink rate**: Studies show that people blink up to 66% less frequently when using screens. The 20-second break gives your eyes a chance to re-moisten.
- **Prevents fatigue**: Periodic relaxation of the focusing mechanism reduces cumulative visual fatigue over long work sessions.

### How to Implement It

1. **Use Safe Eyes**: This application is specifically designed to enforce break reminders. Configure it to remind you every 20 minutes.
2. **Set a timer**: If you don't have Safe Eyes installed, use any timer or alarm app.
3. **Look out a window**: A window with a distant view is ideal for the 20-second break.
4. **Focus on a distant object**: Pick a specific object 20+ feet away—a tree, a building, or a sign—and focus on its details.
5. **Blink actively**: During your 20-second break, make a conscious effort to blink fully and slowly several times.

### Tips for Sticking with It

- **Don't skip breaks**: Even when you're "in the zone," taking a 20-second break will improve—not hurt—your productivity.
- **Combine with stretching**: Use your break to stand up and stretch your neck and shoulders.
- **Make it a habit**: After a few days of consistent practice, the 20-20-20 rule becomes second nature.

## Ergonomics

Proper ergonomics play a crucial role in reducing eye strain, neck pain, and overall physical discomfort during extended screen use.

### Monitor Position

| Setting | Recommendation |
|---------|---------------|
| **Distance** | 20–26 inches (50–65 cm) from your eyes |
| **Height** | Top of the screen at or slightly below eye level |
| **Tilt** | Slightly tilted back (10–20 degrees) |
| **Position** | Directly in front of you, centered |

### Desk and Chair Setup

1. **Chair height**: Feet should rest flat on the floor with thighs parallel to the ground.
2. **Arm position**: Elbows should be at approximately 90 degrees when typing.
3. **Desk height**: The desk should allow your forearms to rest comfortably while typing.
4. **Document holder**: If referencing documents, place them at the same height and distance as your monitor to avoid constant refocusing.

### Lighting and Glare

- **Avoid direct light sources** behind or in front of your screen.
- **Position your monitor perpendicular to windows** to minimize glare.
- **Use anti-glare screen filters** if you cannot eliminate glare through positioning.
- **Adjust blinds or curtains** to control natural light throughout the day.
- **Use indirect or diffused lighting** rather than harsh overhead fluorescent lights.
- **Consider a desk lamp with adjustable brightness** for task lighting.

### Workspace Organization

- Keep your most frequently used items within arm's reach to avoid repetitive stretching.
- Use a monitor arm or stand for flexible positioning.
- If using multiple monitors, place the primary monitor directly in front and the secondary at a slight angle.
- Ensure your keyboard and mouse are at the same level to prevent wrist strain.

## Screen Settings

Optimizing your screen settings can significantly reduce eye strain without requiring any changes to your work habits.

### Brightness

- **Match ambient lighting**: Your screen brightness should be similar to the brightness of your surrounding workspace. A screen that is too bright or too dim causes your eyes to work harder.
- **Auto-brightness**: Enable auto-brightness on devices that support it so the screen adjusts to changing light conditions throughout the day.
- **Avoid working in complete darkness**: A bright screen in a dark room creates extreme contrast that strains your eyes.

### Color Temperature

- **Use warm color tones**: Blue light is high-energy and contributes to eye strain and sleep disruption. Warmer (yellower) color temperatures are easier on the eyes.
- **Night Light / Night Shift**: Enable built-in blue light filters on your operating system:
  - **Windows**: Settings → System → Display → Night light
  - **macOS**: System Preferences → Displays → Night Shift
  - **Linux**: Use Redshift, GNOME Night Light, or f.lux
- **Timing**: Schedule your blue light filter to activate automatically in the evening (sunset to sunrise).

### Text Size and Contrast

- **Increase text size**: If you find yourself squinting or leaning forward, increase your font size. A comfortable reading size prevents unnecessary eye strain.
- **High contrast themes**: Use dark text on a light background or light text on a dark background. Avoid low-contrast color combinations.
- **Browser zoom**: Use `Ctrl +` / `Cmd +` to zoom in on web pages rather than straining to read small text.
- **Minimum font recommendation**: 12pt for body text, larger if you experience any difficulty reading.

### Refresh Rate and Flicker

- **Refresh rate**: Use a monitor with at least 60Hz refresh rate. Higher refresh rates (120Hz, 144Hz) provide smoother motion and may reduce eye strain.
- **PWM flicker**: Some displays use Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) for brightness control, which can cause invisible flickering. Look for flicker-free monitors.
- **Reduce animations**: Minimize unnecessary screen animations that can cause visual fatigue.

### Resolution and Scaling

- **Native resolution**: Always use your monitor's native resolution for the sharpest text rendering.
- **Display scaling**: If text appears too small at native resolution, use OS-level display scaling (125%, 150%) rather than lowering the resolution.

## Long-Term Habits

Protecting your eye health is not just about immediate fixes—it requires building sustainable habits over time.

### Regular Eye Exams

- **Annual comprehensive eye exams**: Visit an optometrist or ophthalmologist at least once a year, even if you don't notice vision problems.
- **Discuss screen use**: Tell your eye care professional about your daily screen time so they can check for digital eye strain specifically.
- **Computer glasses**: Consider getting prescription glasses designed for the specific distance of your computer screen, even if you don't need everyday corrective lenses.
- **Anti-reflective coating**: If you wear glasses, anti-reflective coating reduces glare from screens and overhead lighting.

### The 20-20-20 Rule as a Lifestyle

- **Consistency is key**: The 20-20-20 rule only works if you practice it consistently every day.
- **Safe Eyes as your companion**: Configure Safe Eyes to enforce regular breaks. Start with 20-minute intervals and adjust as needed.
- **Track your breaks**: Some apps can log your break compliance, helping you build the habit.

### Hydration and Nutrition

- **Stay hydrated**: Dehydration reduces tear production, leading to dry eyes. Aim for at least 8 glasses of water daily.
- **Omega-3 fatty acids**: Found in fish, flaxseed, and walnuts, omega-3s support tear production and eye health.
- **Vitamin A**: Essential for retinal health. Found in carrots, sweet potatoes, and leafy greens.
- **Lutein and zeaxanthin**: These antioxidants protect the macula. Found in spinach, kale, and eggs.
- **Vitamin C and E**: Antioxidants that may help prevent cataracts and macular degeneration.

### Physical Activity

- **Regular exercise**: Improves blood circulation, including to the eyes, which helps deliver nutrients and remove waste products.
- **Eye exercises**: While not a substitute for the 20-20-20 rule, exercises like "palming" (covering eyes with warm palms) and "figure-eight" (tracing a large figure-eight with your eyes) can provide relief.
- **Outdoor time**: Spending at least 2 hours outdoors daily has been shown to reduce the risk of myopia, especially in children and young adults.

### Sleep and Recovery

- **7–9 hours of sleep**: Your eyes need rest to recover from daily strain. Adequate sleep allows tear films to replenish and eye muscles to fully relax.
- **Avoid screens before bed**: Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production. Stop using screens at least 1 hour before bedtime.
- **Use dark mode at night**: If you must use screens in the evening, switch to dark mode and reduce brightness to minimum comfortable levels.

### Managing Dry Eyes

- **Blink consciously**: Make a conscious effort to blink fully and frequently, especially when reading or typing on a screen.
- **Use artificial tears**: Over-the-counter lubricating eye drops can help relieve dryness. Choose preservative-free options for frequent use.
- **Humidify your workspace**: Dry air exacerbates dry eye symptoms. Use a humidifier, especially in air-conditioned or heated environments.
- **Take longer breaks**: If your eyes feel persistently dry, take a 5–10 minute break instead of just 20 seconds.

### Know the Warning Signs

Consult an eye care professional if you experience:

- Persistent eye pain or discomfort that doesn't improve with rest
- Frequent headaches associated with screen use
- Blurred or double vision
- Excessive tearing or dryness
- Increased sensitivity to light
- Difficulty focusing between near and far objects

## About Safe Eyes

[Safe Eyes](https://github.com/slgobinath/safeeyes) is a free and open-source application that protects your eyes from eye strain by reminding you to take breaks. It is designed to help you implement the practices described in this guide—especially the 20-20-20 rule—by providing configurable break reminders that you can't easily ignore.

### Recommended Safe Eyes Settings for Eye Health

- **Short break interval**: Every 20 minutes
- **Short break duration**: 20 seconds minimum
- **Long break interval**: Every 60 minutes
- **Long break duration**: 5 minutes
- **Enable strict break**: Prevents skipping breaks for maximum benefit
