## The 20-20-20 Rule

The **20-20-20 rule** is the single most recommended practice by ophthalmologists for reducing digital eye strain (also known as Computer Vision Syndrome). The rule is simple:

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **Every 20 minutes** | Take a break from your screen |
| **Look at something 20 feet (6 meters) away** | Shift your focus to a distant object |
| **For at least 20 seconds** | Allow your eyes to fully relax and refocus |

### Why It Works

- **Relaxes the ciliary muscle:** When you focus on a near object for extended periods, the ciliary muscle in your eye remains contracted. Looking at a distant object allows this muscle to relax.
- **Reduces accommodative spasm:** Prolonged near-focus can cause the focusing mechanism to "lock up," making distance vision blurry temporarily. Regular breaks prevent this.
- **Restores blink rate:** Studies show that people blink 60–70% less when using screens. The 20-20-20 break naturally encourages more frequent blinking, helping to rewet the ocular surface.

### Tips for Implementing the 20-20-20 Rule

1. **Use Safe Eyes** — Configure it to remind you every 20 minutes. The short breaks in Safe Eyes are perfectly suited for this rule.
2. **Look out a window** — Position your workspace near a window so you have a convenient distant view.
3. **Set a far reference point** — If you don't have a window, pick the farthest object in your room (a clock on a far wall, a poster, etc.).
4. **Combine with stretching** — Use the 20-second break to stand up and stretch your neck and shoulders.
5. **Don't skip it** — Even if you're "in the zone," those 20 seconds can save you from hours of discomfort later.

## Ergonomics

Proper workspace ergonomics play a critical role in reducing eye strain, neck pain, and fatigue. Here's how to set up your workstation for optimal eye health:

### Monitor Position

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Distance** | 20–26 inches (50–66 cm) from your eyes |
| **Height** | Top of the screen at or slightly below eye level |
| **Tilt** | Tilt the screen back 10–20 degrees |
| **Position** | Directly in front of you, not off to the side |

### Why Monitor Position Matters

- **Too close:** Forces your eyes to work harder to focus, increasing strain.
- **Too high:** You tilt your head back, exposing more of the eye surface to the air and increasing dryness. It also strains neck muscles.
- **Too low:** Causes you to hunch forward, leading to neck and back pain, and reduces blink rate.
- **Off-center:** Creates uneven visual demand, causing one eye to work harder than the other.

### Lighting and Glare

- **Avoid placing your monitor directly in front of or behind a window.** The contrast between the bright window and the screen increases strain.
- **Use indirect lighting** where possible. Overhead fluorescent lights are a common source of glare.
- **Position your desk perpendicular to windows** to minimize reflections.
- **Use an anti-glare screen filter** if you cannot eliminate glare through positioning.
- **Adjust window coverings** — blinds or curtains can help control ambient light levels.

### Desk and Chair

- Your chair should support your lower back and allow your feet to rest flat on the floor.
- Armrests should support your elbows at roughly 90 degrees, keeping your forearms parallel to the floor while typing.
- Your desk height should allow your shoulders to remain relaxed — not hunched or elevated.

### Document Placement

- If you reference documents frequently, use a document holder positioned next to your monitor at the same height and distance. This reduces the need for your eyes to constantly refocus between near and far distances.

## Screen Settings

Your display settings have a direct impact on visual comfort. Here's how to optimize them:

### Brightness

- **Match your screen brightness to the ambient light level.** Your screen should not look like a light source, nor should it look gray and dull.
- A simple test: hold a piece of white paper next to your screen. The screen's white should approximate the brightness of the paper.
- **Adjust throughout the day** as natural light changes. Consider using automatic brightness adjustment tools.

### Contrast

- Set contrast to a comfortable level — typically 60–70% for LCD monitors.
- Black text on a white background is generally the easiest to read. Avoid low-contrast color schemes for extended reading.

### Color Temperature

- **Blue light** (high color temperature, ~6500K) suppresses melatonin production and can disrupt sleep patterns.
- **Use warmer color temperatures in the evening** (~2700K–3500K) to reduce blue light exposure.
- Tools like **Redshift**, **f.lux**, or **Night Light** (built into most OS) can automatically adjust color temperature based on time of day.

### Text Size and Clarity

- Increase text size so you can read comfortably without leaning forward. A good rule: if you have to squint or lean in, the text is too small.
- Use **ClearType** (Windows), **font smoothing** (macOS), or **subpixel rendering** (Linux) to sharpen on-screen text.
- Set your monitor to its **native resolution** to avoid blurriness from scaling.

### Refresh Rate

- Use the highest available refresh rate for your monitor (60 Hz minimum; 120 Hz+ is preferable).
- A low refresh rate can cause imperceptible flickering that contributes to eye fatigue and headaches.

### Multiple Monitors

- If you use multiple monitors, ensure they are:
  - At the same height and distance
  - Calibrated to similar brightness and color settings
  - Positioned to minimize head turning (primary monitor centered, secondary close at hand)

### Dark Mode

- Dark mode (light text on dark background) can reduce glare in low-light environments but may increase strain for some users due to the halation effect (light text bleeding into the dark background).
- Experiment and choose what feels most comfortable for your eyes and lighting conditions.

## Long-Term Habits

Protecting your vision requires more than just adjusting your setup. These long-term habits are essential for sustained eye health:

### Regular Eye Exams

- **Get a comprehensive eye exam at least every 1–2 years**, even if you think your vision is fine.
- Tell your optometrist or ophthalmologist how many hours per day you spend on screens — they can check for screen-related issues specifically.
- If you need glasses, ask about **computer glasses** with anti-reflective coating, or progressive lenses designed for the intermediate viewing distance of a monitor.

### Blink Awareness

- Normal blink rate: ~15–20 blinks per minute
- Screen use blink rate: ~5–7 blinks per minute
- **Consciously blink more often** when using screens. Practice full blinks (squeeze gently for a moment) to express oils from the meibomian glands.
- If you experience chronic dryness, ask your doctor about **lubricating eye drops** (preservative-free recommended for frequent use).

### The 20-20-20 Rule + Longer Breaks

- In addition to the 20-20-20 micro-breaks, **take a 5–10 minute break every hour** to walk around, stretch, and give your eyes an extended rest.
- Use Safe Eyes' **long break** feature for this purpose — configure it to trigger every 60 minutes.

### Nutrition for Eye Health

| Nutrient | Benefits | Food Sources |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Lutein & Zeaxanthin** | Filters harmful blue light, protects the macula | Kale, spinach, broccoli, eggs |
| **Omega-3 Fatty Acids** | Supports tear production, reduces dry eye | Salmon, sardines, flaxseed, walnuts |
| **Vitamin A** | Essential for retina function, night vision | Carrots, sweet potatoes, liver |
| **Vitamin C** | Antioxidant, may reduce cataract risk | Citrus fruits, bell peppers, strawberries |
| **Vitamin E** | Protects eye cells from oxidative damage | Almonds, sunflower seeds, avocados |
| **Zinc** | Helps transport vitamin A to the retina | Oysters, beef, pumpkin seeds |

### Stay Hydrated

- Dehydration reduces tear production, worsening dry eye symptoms.
- Aim for at least 8 glasses of water per day, and more if you're in a dry or air-conditioned environment.

### Sleep

- **Get 7–9 hours of quality sleep per night.** Your eyes need this time to rest, recover, and replenish tears.
- Avoid screens for at least 30–60 minutes before bedtime to allow melatonin production to begin.
- If you must use a screen before bed, use the warmest possible color temperature setting and keep brightness low.

### Exercise and the 20-20-20-20 Rule

- Some eye care professionals recommend the **20-20-20-20 rule**: every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds, and **blink 20 times**.
- Incorporate the **palming exercise**: rub your palms together to warm them, then gently cup them over your closed eyes for 30 seconds. This relaxes the eye muscles and provides a moment of total darkness.

### Manage Your Environment

- Use a **humidifier** in dry environments to prevent excessive tear evaporation.
- Avoid sitting directly in the path of air conditioning vents or fans, which can dry out your eyes.
- If you wear contact lenses, consider switching to glasses during long screen sessions — contacts can exacerbate dryness.

### Know the Warning Signs

Seek professional eye care if you experience:

- Persistent dryness, burning, or itching
- Frequent headaches after screen use
- Blurred or double vision that doesn't resolve with rest
- Increased sensitivity to light
- Difficulty focusing between near and far objects
- Eye pain or pressure

Early intervention can prevent minor issues from becoming chronic conditions.

## Quick Reference Checklist

Use this daily checklist to maintain healthy screen habits:

- [ ] Safe Eyes is running with 20-minute short breaks and 60-minute long breaks
- [ ] Monitor is 20–26 inches away, with the top at or slightly below eye level
- [ ] Screen brightness matches room lighting
- [ ] Color temperature is adjusted for the time of day
- [ ] No glare on the screen from windows or overhead lights
- [ ] Text size is comfortable without squinting or leaning
- [ ] Following the 20-20-20 rule consistently
- [ ] Blinking consciously during screen use
- [ ] Taking a 5–10 minute break every hour
- [ ] Drinking water throughout the day
- [ ] Avoiding screens 30–60 minutes before bed
- [ ] Scheduling an annual eye exam
